    Section 2.2 Matrix representations of transformations

    Abstract. A linear transformation can be represented in terms of multiplication by a matrix.
    Suppose \(V = \mathbb R^n, W = \mathbb R^m\text{,}\) and \(L_A:V\to W\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} L_A({\bf v}) = A*{\bf v} \end{equation*}
    for some \(m\times n\) real matrix \(A\text{.}\) Then it follows immediately from the properties of matrix algebra that \(L_A\) is a linear transformation:
    \begin{equation*} L_A(\alpha {\bf v} + \beta {\bf w}) = A*(\alpha {\bf v} + \beta {\bf w}) = \alpha (A*{\bf v}) + \beta (A*{\bf w}) = \alpha L_A({\bf v}) + \beta L_A({\bf w}) \end{equation*}
    Conversely, suppose the linear transformation \(L\) is given. Define the matrix \(A_L\) by
    \begin{equation*} A_L = [L({\bf e}_1)\ L({\bf e}_2)\ \dots L({\bf e}_n)] \end{equation*}
    that is, the \(m\times n\) matrix with \(A(:,i) = L({\bf e}_i),\, 1\le i\le n\text{.}\) Then by construction
    \begin{equation*} A_L*({\bf e}_i) = A(:,i) = L({\bf e}_i),\, 1\le i\le n \end{equation*}
    so that \({\bf v}\mapsto L({\bf v})\) and \({\bf v}\mapsto A_L*{\bf v}\) are two linear transformations which agree on a basis for \(\mathbb R^n\text{,}\) which by the previous corollary implies
    \begin{equation*} L({\bf v}) = A_L*({\bf v})\qquad \forall {\bf v}\in \mathbb R^n \end{equation*}
    Because of this, the matrix \(A_L\) is referred to as a matrix representation of \(L\text{.}\) Note that this representation is with respect to to the standard basis for \(\mathbb R^n\) and \(\mathbb R^m\text{.}\)
    We see now that the same type of representation applies for arbitrary vector spaces once a basis has been fixed for both the domain and target. In other words, given
    • A vector space \(V\) with basis \(S = \{ {\bf v}_1,\dots ,{\bf v}_n\} \text{,}\)
    • a vector space \(W\) with basis \(T = \{ {\bf w}_1,\dots ,{\bf w}_m\} \text{,}\) and
    • a linear transformation \(L:V\to W\)
    we could ask if there is a similar representation of \(L\) in terms of a matrix (which depends on these two choices of bases). The answer is "yes".

    Theorem 2.2.1.

    For any \({\bf v}\in V\)
    \begin{equation*} {}_TL({\bf v}) = {}_TL_S*{}_S{\bf v} \end{equation*}
    where \({}_TL_S\) is the \(m\times n\) matrix defined by
    \begin{equation*} {}_TL_S = [{}_TL({\bf v}_1)\ {}_TL({\bf v}_2)\ \dots \ {}_TL({\bf v}_n)] \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Again by the above corollary it suffices to verify the equality for basis vectors. But \({}_S{\bf v}_i\) is the \(n\times 1\) coordinate vector identical to the basis vector \({\bf e}_i\) for \(\mathbb R^n\text{.}\) From this we get
    \begin{equation*} {}_TL({\bf v}_i) = {}_TL_S(:,i) = {}_TL_S*{}_S{\bf v}_i,\qquad 1\le i\le n \end{equation*}
    completing the proof.

    Example 2.2.2.

    Suppose \(L:\mathbb R^3\to \mathbb R^2\) is the linear transformation given by
    \begin{equation*} L({\bf x}) = L\left(\begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ x_3\end{bmatrix}\right) = \begin{bmatrix} 2x_1 - 3x_2 + 7x_3\\ 4x_2 - 5x_3\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    To compute the matrix representation of \(L\) with respect to the standard bases of \(\mathbb R^3\) and \(\mathbb R^2\text{,}\) we evaluate \(L\) on the basis vectors \({\bf e}_i, 1\le i\le 3\) and then form the matrix \(A_L = \begin{bmatrix} {L(\bf e}_1) \amp L({\bf e}_2) \amp L({\bf e}_3)\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) yielding
    \begin{equation*} A_L = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -3 \amp 7\\ 0 \amp 4 \amp -5 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    Example 2.2.3.

    Recall that \(P_n\) is the vector space of polynomials in one variable with real coefficients. Let \(L:P_3\to P_3\) be given by \(L(p(x)) = 2p'(x) - p(x)\text{,}\) where \(p'(x)\) denotes the derivative of \(p(x)\text{.}\) To find the matrix representation of \(L\) with respect to the basis \(\{ 1,x,x^2\} \) for \(P_3\text{,}\) we first compute \(L(1) = -1; L(x) = 2 - 2x; L(x^2) = 4x - x^2\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} A_L = \begin{bmatrix} L(1) \amp L(x) \amp L(x^2)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \amp 2 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 4\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Returning once more to the general case where \(L:V\to W\) is linear, \(S\) a basis for \(V\text{,}\) \(T\) a basis for \(W\text{,}\) we note that the bases \(S\) and \(T\) can be used to identify the kernel and image of \(L\text{.}\) Precisely, we have

    Theorem 2.2.4.

    Assume \(Dim(V) = n\text{,}\) \(Dim(W) = m\text{.}\) Let \(A = {}_{T}L_{S}\) be the \(m\times n\) matrix representation of \(L\) with respect to the pair of bases \(S,T\text{.}\) Then
    • \({\bf v}\in ker(L)\subset V\) iff \({}_{S}{\bf v}\in N(A)\subset \mathbb R^n\text{.}\)
    • \({\bf w}\in im(L)\subset W\) iff \({}_{T}{\bf w}\in C(A)\subset \mathbb R^m\text{.}\)
    This theorem tells us that, once we have fixed a basis for \(V\) and \(W\text{,}\) the representation of \(L\) by the matrix \(A={}_{T}L_{S}\) further identifies i) the kernel \(ker(L)\) of \(L\) with the nullspace \(N(A)\) of \(A\) and ii) the image \(im(L)\) with the column space \(C(A)\) of \(A\text{.}\)

    Example 2.2.5.

    Suppose \(L: P_3\to P_3\) is the linear transformation represented with respect to the standard basis on \(P_3\) by the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 3 \amp 1\\ 3 \amp 9 \amp 6\\ 1 \amp 6 \amp 5\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Our objective is to find a minimal spanning set \(ker(L)\) and \(im(L)\) (with respect to the standard basis for \(P^3\)). First we compute \(rref(A) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp -1\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) We then see
    • The column space \(C(A)\) of \(A\) is \(Span\{ A(:,1), A(:,2)\} = Span\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ 3\\ \mathbf{1}\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 3\\ 9\\ 6\end{bmatrix}\right\} \text{;}\)
    • the nullspace \(N(A)\) of \(A\) is \(Span\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ -1\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\} \text{.}\) Note that these are coordinate vectors.
    • The corresponding vectors, written as polynomials in \(P_3\text{,}\) give
      \begin{equation*} im(L) = Span\left\{ (2 + 3x + x^2), (3 + 9x + 6x^2)\right\} , \quad ker(L) = Span\left\{ (1 - x + x^2)\right\} \end{equation*}

    Checkpoint 2.2.6.

    Suppose \(L:P_4\to P_4\) is the linear transformation whose representation in the standard basis for \(P_4\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} A =\begin{bmatrix} 10 \amp -16 \amp -2 \amp -2\\ -16 \amp 36 \amp 22 \amp 12\\ -2 \amp 22 \amp 53 \amp -4\\ -2 \amp 12 \amp -4 \amp 30\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Following the method in the above example, write down a minimal spanning set for \(ker(L)\) and \(im(L)\) (the elements should be vectors in \(P_4\) - i.e., polynomials, not their coordinate representations.)
