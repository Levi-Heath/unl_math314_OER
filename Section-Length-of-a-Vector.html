    Section 1.3 Length of a Vector

    Vector quantities, such as velocity and force, have magnitude and direction. The magnitude of a vector quantity is the length of the vector. For example, if a force of 10 Newtons is applied to an object, we would represent the force by a 10-unit-long vector.
    The magnitude of a vector is denoted by double absolute value brackets. In the case of force \(\mathbf{F}\text{,}\) we write
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{F}}=10 \end{equation*}
    To find the length of a vector, we need to find the distance between the tail of the vector and its head. Recall that in \(\R^2\text{,}\) the distance between \(A(a_1, a_2)\) and \(B(b_1, b_2)\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} d_{AB}=\sqrt{(a_1-b_1)^2+(a_2-b_2)^2} \end{equation*}
    A vector \(\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}v_1\\ v_2\end{bmatrix}\) has the length of the vector in standard position with its head at \((v_1, v_2)\) and tail at \((0, 0)\text{.}\) We find the length of \(\mathbf{v}\) using the distance formula
    \begin{equation} \norm{\mathbf{v}}=\sqrt{(v_1-0)^2+(v_2-0)^2}=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2} \tag{1.3.1} \end{equation}

    Example 1.3.1.

    Find the magnitude of \(\mathbf{u}=\begin{bmatrix}-3\\4\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{u}}=\sqrt{(-3)^2+(4)^2}=5 \end{equation*}
    The distance formula for points in \(\R^3\) is analogous to the distance formula in \(\R^2\text{.}\) Given two points \(A(a_1, a_2, a_3)\) and \(B(b_1, b_2, b_3)\text{,}\) the distance between them is given by
    \begin{equation*} d_{AB}=\sqrt{(a_1-b_1)^2+(a_2-b_2)^2+(a_3-b_3)^2} \end{equation*}
    To find the length of vector \(\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}v_1\\ v_2\\ v_3\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) we find the distance between \((v_1, v_2, v_3)\) and \((0, 0, 0)\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation} \norm{\mathbf{v}}=\sqrt{(v_1-0)^2+(v_2-0)^2+(v_3-0)^2}=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2}\tag{1.3.2} \end{equation}

    Example 1.3.2.

    The magnitude of \(\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}6\\-2\\3\end{bmatrix}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{v}}=\sqrt{(6)^2+(-2)^2+(3)^2}=7 \end{equation*}
    Distance formulas for \(\R^2\) and \(\R^3\) motivate the following definition of distance between two points in \(\R^n\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.3.3.

    Let \(A(a_1, a_2,\ldots, a_n)\) and \(B(b_1, b_2,\ldots, b_n)\) be points in \(\R^n\text{.}\) The distance between \(A\) and \(B\) is defined to be
    \begin{equation*} d_{AB}=\sqrt{(a_1-b_1)^2+(a_2-b_2)^2+\ldots +(a_n-b_n)^2} \end{equation*}
    The following definition follows directly from the distance formula for \(\R^n\) in the same way that expressions ((1.3.1) and (1.3.2) followed from distance formulas in \(\R^2\) and \(\R^3\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.3.4.

    Let \(\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}v_1\\ v_2\\ \vdots \\v_n\end{bmatrix}\) be a vector in \(\R^n\text{,}\) then the length, or the magnitude, of \(\mathbf{v}\) is given by
    \begin{equation} \norm{\mathbf{v}}=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+\ldots +v_n^2}\tag{1.3.3} \end{equation}

    Example 1.3.5.

    The magnitude of \(\mathbf{w}=\begin{bmatrix}5\\ -1\\ -2\\ 4\end{bmatrix}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{w}}=\sqrt{5^2+(-1)^2+(-2)^2+4^2}=\sqrt{46} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.3.1 Practice Problems

    Exercises Exercises

    Exercise Group.
    Find the length of the following vectors.
    1.
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{u}}=\sqrt{10} \end{equation*}
    2.
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}-4\\-6\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{v}}= 2\sqrt{13} \end{equation*}
    3.
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{w}=\begin{bmatrix}2\\-4\\6\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{w}}=\answer{2}\sqrt{\answer{14}} \end{equation*}
    4.
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{q}=\begin{bmatrix}-10\\ 4\\ -7\\ 5\\ 3\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \norm{\mathbf{q}}=\sqrt{199} \end{equation*}
    5.
    Find the component form of vector \(\mathbf{v}\) in \(\R^2\) if we know that \(\norm{\mathbf{v}}=15\text{,}\) the \(x\) component of \(\mathbf{v}\) is \(-9\) and the vector is located in the third quadrant.
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix} -9\\ -12\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    6.
    For a vector in \(\R^2\) with a length of 26 and the \(y\) component of 10, what are the possibilities for the \(x\) component?
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} -24 \quad\text{and}\quad 24 \end{equation*}
    7.
    Let \(\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\ y\\ 4\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Find all possible values of \(y\) if \(\norm{\mathbf{v}}=9\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} -8 \quad\text{and}\quad 8 \end{equation*}
    8.
    For a vector in \(\R^4\text{,}\) what are the possibilities for the fourth component if the length of the vector is 14, and the \(x\text{,}\) \(y\) and \(z\) components are 1, 5 and 13, respectively?
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} -1 \quad\text{and}\quad 1 \end{equation*}
    9.
    The points \(A(2,1,4),B(4,2,-1)\text{,}\) and \(C(6,8,7)\) form a triangle in \(\R^3\text{.}\) Is it a right triangle?
    Hint.
    Does the Pythagorean Theorem hold?
